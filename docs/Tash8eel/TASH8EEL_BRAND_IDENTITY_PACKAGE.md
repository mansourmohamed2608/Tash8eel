## SECTION 1 — BRAND IDENTITY SUMMARY

### What Kind of SaaS Brand Tash8heel AI Is

Tash8heel AI is an **operational infrastructure brand** — not a software product brand, not an AI startup brand, not a consumer app brand.

The closest accurate frame: it is the brand of a serious system that runs a business. The brand signals that the product is the infrastructure layer of a merchant's daily operations. It is built, not pitched. It works, not impresses.

The identity sits in the same register as Stripe, Rippling, and Linear — products that earn trust through precision and restraint, not through emotional marketing or visual excitement. But Tash8heel is calibrated further toward operational weight because its buyer is an Egyptian chain operator with real skepticism toward software hype.

### What It Should Feel Like

- A system someone would trust with their business on day one
- Capable and serious without feeling cold or institutional
- Modern without feeling like a startup
- Arabic-native without being decorative or folkloric
- Intelligent without announcing intelligence constantly
- Reliable enough that you'd show it to a business partner

### What It Should Not Feel Like

- An AI company (gradient orbs, dark control rooms, glowing panels, teal-everything)
- A consumer app (rounded everything, warm pastels, friendly illustrations, playful motion)
- A traditional ERP (gray, dense, table-heavy, circa 2008 design language)
- A regional tech product (imitating Foodics, Salla, or any existing Egyptian SaaS visual language)
- A startup in pitch mode (excitement, exclamation marks, launch-energy copy)
- A product that trades on the word "AI" before proving the product

## SECTION 2 — FINAL BRAND PERSONALITY

### Core Personality Traits (Fixed)

**Composed** — the brand does not raise its voice. It speaks from a position of quiet certainty.  
**Precise** — every visual, copy, and interaction decision is deliberate. Nothing is approximate.  
**Serious** — not joyless, but never jokey. The product handles real money and real operations.  
**Credible** — the identity should make an Egyptian operator feel they can trust this system with their livelihood.  
**Restrained** — what is left out is as important as what is included. No decoration that has not earned its place.

### Operational Personality

The product exists to run a business. This must be visible in the brand:

- The brand does not use lifestyle imagery. It shows operational reality.
- The brand does not celebrate features. It demonstrates function.
- The brand does not chase trends. It builds toward permanence.
- The brand voice sounds like a senior operations expert, not a growth marketer.

### Premium Personality

Premium is communicated through **restraint and precision**, not through luxury signals:

- Clean typographic hierarchy
- Deliberate use of white space (never forced spaciousness)
- A color system that earns trust through consistency, not through beauty
- No gold, no decoration, no luxury-brand language — premium here means serious, not expensive

### Intelligence Personality

The intelligence of the product must be visible but not theatrical:

- AI contributions are surfaced with a consistent, distinct visual signal (teal as semantic AI marker)
- AI is never the loudest thing on a page
- The brand never uses the word "AI" as decoration — AI appears when it is doing something verifiable
- Intelligence is expressed through quality of information design, not through AI-themed visual language

### Arabic-Market Fit

This is not a brand that has been "Arabized." It is a brand that was built for Arabic-speaking, Egyptian operators as its primary context:

- RTL is the default reading order, not an adaptation
- The word تشغيل carries brand meaning — it is operational, active, grounded
- The register is professional Egyptian Arabic in daily product copy — not MSA formality, not Western approximation
- Trust is built slowly, through proof — the brand does not oversell on first contact
- The buyer is experienced and skeptical — the brand respects their intelligence

## SECTION 3 — FINAL BRAND VOICE

### Voice Characteristics

**Composed authority** — speaks from certainty, not from enthusiasm  
**Operator-first** — language of the business owner and operations director, not the developer or marketer  
**Evidence before claim** — shows what the system does before declaring what it is  
**Spare** — removes every word that does not earn its place  
**Direct** — the most important thing comes first, always

### Writing Style

- Sentence-first structure: the core idea in the opening clause, every time
- Short paragraphs. Short sentences. No multi-clause winding.
- Concrete over abstract: "your branches share one order queue" not "unified operational architecture"
- Active voice always in product copy and marketing
- Numbered lists only when sequence matters. Bullets only when parallel. Never for everything.
- No padding phrases: "In today's fast-moving world…", "At Tash8heel, we believe…", "Powered by cutting-edge AI…"

### Arabic Tone Rules

1. Egyptian Arabic register for marketing headlines, product microcopy, and social content
2. MSA for contracts, compliance documents, and formal enterprise proposals
3. Short. Active. Verb-first when possible.
4. The تشغيل word family is load-bearing — use it. شغّل، تشغيل، يشتغل، مشتغل
5. Never hyperbolic. Egyptian business buyers have heard every claim. Speak to them as peers.
6. Direct benefit before feature. Not "نظام يدعم الإشعارات الفورية" — instead "بتعرف في اللحظة لما في مشكلة"
7. No exclamation marks in brand copy. The product does not need to shout.
8. RTL sentence logic: most important claim lands at the start of the Arabic sentence.

### English Tone Rules

1. Operator language, not investor language — describe operations, not platform capabilities
2. Sentence-first clarity — never warm up before the main point
3. Proof before claim — show what the system does before naming what it is
4. Short always wins over long for UI copy
5. No passive voice in product-facing text
6. "The system flags…" not "unusual patterns are surfaced by…"
7. Numbers ground claims: "one queue, all channels" not "unified omnichannel experience"

### Words to Use

**In Arabic:** تشغيل، شغّل، نظام، عمليات، فرع، متحكم، واضح، دقيق، تلقائي، تنبيه، مراقبة، بناء، يعمل، شغال  
**In English:** system, operations, branch, control, clear, real-time, unified, track, automate, brief, alert, signal, accountable, built, runs

### Words to Avoid

**In Arabic:** ذكاء اصطناعي (as a lead claim), مميز، حلول، منصة متكاملة، رائدة في المجال، متطور  
**In English:** AI-powered (as opening claim), solution, platform, seamless, cutting-edge, intelligent, next-generation, revolutionary, game-changing, disrupting, unlock  
**Never in any language:** "All-in-one platform for…", "Leverage the power of…", "Supercharge your…", "The future of…"

## SECTION 4 — TAGLINE / SLOGAN SYSTEM

### What Kind of Tagline Direction Fits This Product

Not a clever wordplay tagline. Not an aspirational lifestyle tagline. Not an AI-feature tagline.

The tagline should communicate: **this system runs your business, with intelligence underneath.** It should be short enough to be remembered, operational enough to signal the product category, and credible enough to work before pilots exist.

It must work in both Arabic and English natively — not translated between them.

### Primary Tagline (English)

**"One system. Your whole operation."**

Why this tagline:

- Claims the unification benefit without listing features
- "System" signals infrastructure-grade seriousness
- "Your whole operation" is the buyer's language — they think in terms of their operation
- Understated. Does not oversell.
- Works as a brand line, a demo opener, a website hero, and a deck cover

### Secondary Tagline Options (English)

For specific contexts where the primary tagline needs support or variation:

- **"Built for the way you actually run your business."** — used when the audience has been burned by generic SaaS before
- **"The operating system your branches have been missing."** — used in product-specific contexts, longer form
- **"AI working quietly. Your operations running clearly."** — used when AI needs to be surfaced in marketing, not leading

### Arabic Primary Tagline

**نظام واحد. كل عملياتك.**

Why this is the right Arabic form:

- Direct parallel to English primary — not a translation, same logic
- تشغيل family implied — "عمليات" carries the operational weight
- Short enough for wordmark lockup contexts
- Works as hero copy and deck cover

### Arabic Secondary Options

- **"شغّل نشاطك بنظام واحد"** — more verb-forward, used in action-oriented contexts
- **"شغّال في الخلفية. واضح في النتائج."** — used when AI layer needs to be communicated
- **"من أول يوم، كل شيء في مكانه"** — for onboarding and early-stage positioning contexts

### Tagline Usage Rules

- Never append "powered by AI" to the primary tagline
- The Arabic and English versions run independently — never mix in a single line
- In any RTL context, Arabic tagline is canonical
- Do not change the taglines by context — the system is built to hold one primary line

## SECTION 5 — FINAL COLOR PALETTE

### Color System Philosophy

**Two distinct systems with partial overlap:**

- Brand system: used in marketing, website, collateral, wordmark
- Product system: used inside the product UI

The brand color appears once inside the product — as the interactive affordance color. All other color usage inside the product is semantic (meaning-carrying), not brand-carrying.

---

### Primary Brand Color

**Operational Blue**  
Hex: `#2D6BE4`  
Role: Brand identifier. The single color that makes Tash8heel recognizable.  
Usage: Wordmark, marketing CTAs, website primary actions, selected states, interactive affordances in product  
Never: Used as a background fill inside the product, never as a surface color, never on text

HSL calibration: hue ~220°, sat ~75%, lightness ~53% — sits in the modern-operational register without reading as "startup blue" or "corporate navy"

---

### Secondary Brand Color

**Deep Navy** (context anchor)  
Hex: `#1A2B4A`  
Role: Weight anchor for the brand system — used in wordmark dark variant, dense headline contexts, footer backgrounds in marketing  
Usage: Brand collateral backgrounds, dark-on-light wordmark base, marketing surfaces that need weight  
Never: Used inside the product, never as a dominant surface

---

### Accent Color

**AI Teal** (semantic-only, reserved)  
Hex: `#1AAFA0`

Usage: AI recommendation cards, Copilot interface chrome, AI-surfaced alerts, automation result indicators  
Never: Used in marketing as a brand accent, never used decoratively, never appears without AI meaning

This color must be used consistently and sparingly. If it appears frequently, operators stop reading it as an AI signal.

### Light Mode and Dark Mode Role

**Light mode is the default and primary product identity.** This is not a dark-themed product. The warm off-white system with blue interactive affordances and semantic color is the correct primary product expression.

**Dark mode may be offered later** — specifically for operations managers working in low-light environments (night shifts, back-of-house). When it is built:

- Background becomes `#111318` with surface at `#1C1E23`
- Text palette inverts
- Semantic colors shift to higher-contrast variants
- Brand blue remains the same hex in dark mode — it reads correctly at this saturation level
- AI teal remains the same hex — it is calibrated for both light and dark contexts

Dark mode is never the brand identity. Screenshots, decks, and marketing assets default to light mode.

---

### How Colors Are Used

**Marketing / website:**

- Brand blue for CTAs, links, and key highlights
- Deep navy for weight anchors and contrast sections
- Off-white and white for backgrounds
- Semantic colors are absent in marketing contexts unless showing product screenshots

**Inside the product:**

- Brand blue only for interactive affordances (buttons, links, active states, selected rows)
- Teal only for AI signals — if something is not AI-generated, it does not get teal
- Semantic colors (green, amber, red) for status and meaning only
- The neutral palette carries structure — not the brand palette
- Never use brand blue as a surface fill, panel background, or decorative stripe

## SECTION 6 — TYPOGRAPHY DIRECTION

### Typography Philosophy

Typography is the primary brand vehicle for Tash8heel AI. Because the color system is restrained and the iconography is minimal, the typefaces must carry the seriousness, precision, and credibility of the brand.

Arabic and English type must feel designed together — not selected independently and forced to coexist.

---

### Arabic-First Font Direction

**Primary Arabic typeface: IBM Plex Arabic**

Why IBM Plex Arabic:

- Designed as a professional, functional Arabic type system — not a decorative or heritage typeface
- Optimized for screen readability at both large display sizes and dense table/data contexts
- Has weight range (Regular, Medium, SemiBold) sufficient for the full typographic hierarchy
- Pairs structurally with Latin Plex families — they share proportional logic and were designed in relationship
- Not overused in the Egyptian SaaS market — differentiates without being obscure
- Excellent rendering at small sizes — critical for table-dense product UI

Alternative consideration if licensing or rendering issues emerge: **Noto Sans Arabic** — solid fallback, more neutral, less character. Do not use: Almarai (too consumer-forward), Cairo (overused in Egyptian digital), or any calligraphic Arabic typeface.

---

### English Companion Font Direction

**Primary English typeface: IBM Plex Sans**

Why IBM Plex Sans:

- Designed as the Latin companion to IBM Plex Arabic — they share visual grammar
- Reads as modern-operational without reading as startup (unlike Geist, Inter, or DM Sans)
- Strong at information-dense contexts — tables, labels, data values render cleanly
- Has sufficient weight range for the full hierarchy
- Not overused in the SaaS category enough to be invisible

**Display / headline variant:** IBM Plex Sans **Condensed** for large marketing headlines where character count is high and horizontal economy matters.

---

### Headline Style

**Arabic headlines:**  
IBM Plex Arabic, SemiBold (600), RTL-first, right-anchored  
Tracking: 0 (no artificial letter-spacing — Arabic letterforms do not benefit from it)  
Size range: 32–56px for section headlines, 20–28px for subheadings  
Color: Text Primary (`#1C1E23`) on light backgrounds, Text Inverse on dark

**English headlines:**  
IBM Plex Sans, SemiBold or Condensed SemiBold  
Tracking: -0.01em to -0.02em for large display sizes  
Size range: 36–60px for hero/marketing, 22–32px for section headlines  
Color: Text Primary or Brand Blue for active/selected headline contexts

**Rule:** Headline color is never brand blue decoratively — only when the headline IS a navigation item, a selected state, or a functional signal.

---

### Body Style

**Arabic body:**  
IBM Plex Arabic Regular (400), line-height 1.7–1.8 (Arabic needs more vertical breathing room than Latin)  
Size: 15–16px for main reading; 13–14px for secondary, labels, metadata  
Max-width: 65–70 characters per line in Arabic reading contexts

**English body:**  
IBM Plex Sans Regular (400), line-height 1.6–1.7  
Size: 15–16px for main reading; 13px for metadata and labels  
Max-width: 70–75 characters per line

---

### Product/UI Typography Logic

Inside the product, typography serves information density and operational speed — not brand expression:

- **Table data:** 13–14px, Regular, Neutral 700 — needs to be scannable at speed
- **Status labels / badges:** 11–12px, Medium (500), uppercase NEVER — sentence case always
- **Input fields:** 14–15px, Regular — comfortable for data entry in Arabic and English
- **Navigation items:** 13–14px, Medium — clear but not loud
- **Empty state copy:** 14px, Regular, Neutral 500 — informative, never decorative
- **Error messages:** 13px, Regular, Danger color — short and actionable
- **AI surface copy:** Same size as context, but rendered in AI teal color — the color is the signal, not a different typeface

Type hierarchy in the product must be controlled. No more than four distinct visual sizes in any single view. Emphasis through weight and color — never through decorative treatments.

---

### Brand/Marketing Typography Logic

In marketing contexts (website, deck, social), the typeface can stretch further:

- **Hero type:** IBM Plex Sans Condensed, large, tight — creates presence without luxury-brand excess
- **Pull quotes:** IBM Plex Arabic, larger weight, used sparingly and always sourced from real operator language
- **Data callouts:** Mono-weight numerics from IBM Plex Mono — numbers in brand contexts (statistics, operational figures) benefit from tabular rendering
- **Body in marketing:** Same as product — no decorative serif injection

## SECTION 7 — LOGO / WORDMARK DIRECTION

### What Kind of Logo System Fits Tash8heel AI

**Text-led wordmark with a minimal brand mark.**

Not a symbol-dominant identity — because the product name itself carries meaning (تشغيل = to operate / to run), and that meaning should be visible, not abstracted into a shape. The wordmark is the primary logo expression.

A minimal supporting mark exists for app icons, favicon contexts, and monogram situations — but it never competes with the wordmark.

### What It Should Communicate

- Precision and weight — not friendliness or warmth
- Operational seriousness — not technological excitement
- Arabic-native credibility — the Arabic wordmark is not a transliteration decoration
- Structural modernity — not heritage, not consumer

### Wordmark Construction Logic

**Arabic wordmark:** "تشغيل" rendered in IBM Plex Arabic SemiBold. RTL. The letterforms should be set tightly but not artificially condensed. The dot on the ي should be given visual space — it is a character of the brand name, not an incidental diacritic.

**English wordmark:** "Tash8eel" — the "8" is an intentional brand element, not an error. It references the Arabic letter ع (ain) which is commonly transliterated as "8" in Arabic internet culture. This is culturally legible to the Egyptian and broader Arab digital audience and signals authenticity.

The "8" should be set in the same weight as the surrounding characters — not bolded, not colored differently in default usage. It should read as part of the word, not as a logo trick.

**Arabic + English combined lockup:** Arabic above, English below, right-aligned. Never English above Arabic. In RTL brand contexts, the Arabic wordmark is the primary expression and the English is the secondary.

**Color variants:**

- Light context: Operational Blue (`#2D6BE4`) wordmark — primary brand expression
- Dark context: White wordmark on Deep Navy background
- Monochrome: Full black or full white — no color reduction that loses brand recognition
- Single-color print: Full black

### What Logo Directions to Avoid

- No logomark with a brain, circuit board, or neural network visual
- No AI-wave, signal pulse, or data-flow logo language
- No Arabic calligraphic flourish treatment — this is an operational brand, not a heritage brand
- No gradient wordmark
- No symbol that looks like an "A" or gear or building — these carry no specific meaning for this product
- No combined "T+8" monogram that loses the Arabic name's visibility

### The Supporting Mark (App Icon / Favicon Context)

The mark is a single character: **"ت"** — the first letter of تشغيل — set in IBM Plex Arabic SemiBold, on a solid Operational Blue background, with corner rounding appropriate to the app icon context (square with rounded corners for iOS/Android, circle for some web contexts).

This mark must be instantly recognizable as Arabic, immediately legible at small sizes, and unambiguous — it is not a stylized abstract shape.

## SECTION 8 — ICONOGRAPHY / VISUAL MOTIF DIRECTION

### Icon Style

**System:** Outlined, single-weight line icons. Not filled. Not duotone. Not illustrated.

The icon system communicates function. It does not decorate.

Reference aesthetic: Linear Icons or Phosphor Icons in their outlined weight — consistent stroke width, no variable-stroke decorative treatment, no filled elements mixed with outlined elements.

**Specific calibration:**

- Stroke weight: 1.5px at 24×24px canvas — consistent across the entire set
- Corner treatment: Slightly rounded corners (2px radius) — not sharp, not aggressively rounded
- Fill: Never on standard operational icons
- Exception: Status icons (success checkmark, warning triangle, error circle) may use a filled variant when they appear in badge or status-pill contexts — not in navigation or general UI

### Shape Language

**Geometric and rectilinear.** The brand does not use organic, flowing, or amorphous shapes. Rectangles, squares, and simple geometric derivations with controlled rounding.

This applies to:

- Icons
- Card corners (moderate rounding — not pill-shaped, not sharp)
- Dashboard metric tiles
- Status badges
- Any recurring UI element

The shape language reads as structured and operational. It does not read as soft, playful, or enterprise-heavy.

### Line Weight Feel

**Medium-light.** Not heavy (which reads ERP), not hairline (which reads luxury design tool). A consistent 1.5px stroke at 24px renders as precise and modern.

In marketing contexts, line elements used as dividers or structural accents should match: 1px at most, Neutral 300 color, horizontal only. No decorative diagonal lines, no geometric pattern overlays.

### Visual Motifs and Recurring Brand Forms

**The data grid / table form** — the product is fundamentally a system for seeing and acting on operational data. The visual language of clean rows, clear columns, and structured hierarchy should appear in marketing and collateral as a motif — not as a screenshot, but as a structural element. Thin horizontal rules, aligned columns, precise spacing.

**The status signal** — the three-state system (green/amber/red for healthy/at risk/critical) is a recurring visual form across the product. In brand contexts, these three signals can appear as a simplified reference — not as a traffic light (too literal), but as a row of three minimal dots or bars that communicates system-awareness.

**Operational blue as a precise accent** — brand blue appears as a thin highlight: a left-border accent on a card, the color of an interactive element in a marketing screenshot, the underline of a key metric. Never as a large fill.

### What Should Never Appear

- AI-specific iconography (brain, neural network, chip, robot, circuitry) in any brand context
- Decorative illustrations of any kind — no abstract swirls, no floating geometric shapes, no blob background forms
- Stock photography of smiling people — if photography is ever used in marketing, it should be direct, operational, and contextually specific (an actual merchant environment, an actual operational dashboard)
- Egyptian visual folklore or pattern references — this is not the register of this brand
- Any icon style mixing (filled + outlined in the same view is a failure state)
- Drop shadows on icons or cards in product UI
- Animation on icons used for navigation or status purposes

## SECTION 9 — BRAND RULES

### Do's

1. Let the typography carry the brand weight. The typeface is doing heavy lifting — trust it.
2. Use brand blue exactly once in any single UI view — as the primary CTA or active state.
3. Treat white space as a feature, not wasted real estate.
4. Let status colors do their job. If green appears, it means healthy. Protect that signal.
5. Use teal only when something was generated or surfaced by AI. Always. No exceptions.
6. Write the Arabic copy first. The English should follow the Arabic logic, not the reverse.
7. Show what the system does before claiming what it is.
8. Use IBM Plex Arabic for all Arabic text at every scale — in product and in marketing.
9. Keep marketing screenshots in light mode.
10. Anchor every layout to RTL logic — right side is primary.

### Don'ts

1. Do not use the word "AI" as a marketing opener. It is the second claim, never the first.
2. Do not use gold, amber, or warm yellow as accent or decorative color. Amber means warning. Full stop.
3. Do not use teal in marketing contexts — it has one meaning inside the product and must not be diluted.
4. Do not mix Arabic typefaces. IBM Plex Arabic is the system. Using Cairo for headlines and IBM for body is a failure state.
5. Do not use brand blue as a card background, section fill, or decorative band.
6. Do not use pure white (`#FFFFFF`) as the primary page background inside the product. The warm off-white is the default.
7. Do not animate logo or wordmark.
8. Do not show the dark-themed product as a brand asset. Dark mode is a future product option, not the identity.
9. Do not use more than three type sizes in a single product view.
10. Do not apply drop shadows to cards, panels, or icons in the product UI.
11. Do not use the phrase "AI-powered" in any external-facing brand copy.
12. Do not use exclamation marks in Arabic or English brand copy.

### What Makes the Brand Look Generic

- Using Inter or Roboto for Arabic contexts (Latin font with Arabic glyphs, renders poorly at small sizes)
- Using teal as a general brand accent color (every regional tech brand in MENA uses this)
- Using a brain or neural network visual anywhere near the wordmark
- Mixing two different icon styles in the same view
- Using blue as a panel background instead of an affordance color
- Arabic copy that reads like a translation of English copy rather than natively written Arabic
- Empty states with illustrated characters or emotional imagery
- Headers written in all-caps in either language

### What Makes It Look Too AI-Startup

- Dark product screenshots as primary brand assets
- "Glow" effects on any interface element
- The word "intelligence" used as a marketing headline
- Gradients on the logo or wordmark
- Teal used as a dominant brand color (versus its locked role as AI semantic signal)
- Copy that leads with "The AI that…" or "Powered by AI…"
- A hero section with particle effects, animated mesh backgrounds, or orbital shapes

### What Makes It Look Too ERP-Heavy

- Gray as the dominant neutral — this brand uses warm off-white, not institutional gray
- Dense typography with no breathing room between sections
- Red used for anything other than critical errors
- Table-heavy marketing screenshots without context or annotations
- Icons that look like they belong in Microsoft Dynamics
- Button language like "Submit" or "Process" instead of clear action verbs
- A dark navy or charcoal used as the primary brand surface

### What Makes It Lose Trust (Egypt Context)

- Quantified claims before pilots exist ("reduces overhead by 40%")
- Marketing copy that sounds like a pitch instead of a peer conversation
- A demo environment that loads slowly or shows placeholder data
- Arabic copy with grammatical errors or unnatural phrasing
- English-first collateral with Arabic translation underneath — Arabic should be primary
- Any implication that the AI is making decisions without operator approval
- Onboarding flows that require too many steps before first value is visible
- A pricing page that is hard to understand or has hidden logic
